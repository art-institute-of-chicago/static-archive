Dance, 1932–33

In the 1930s, Matisse returned to the lessons of his years of radical invention, albeit for very different endsheight: 250;. He recovered the clarity of design and reprised his interest in serial images in order to achieve what he called “a certain formal perfection” in his new work. He also revived his bathers motif for a new decorative commission, the greatly simplified Dance, which is connected to Back (IV) in theme and style.

Dance, 1932–33. Oil on canvas. The Barnes Foundation, Merion, Penn.


Matisse’s studio at the Hôtel Régina, Nice, 1953

The plaster of Back (IV) remained in Matisse’s studio for the remainder of his life, no doubt influencing his boldly abstracted and reduced drawings, and especially his monumental paper cutouts, carved from color in a palpably physical way.

Matisse’s studio at the Hôtel Régina, Nice, 1953, with his plaster Back (IV) and a number of his paper cutouts on the walls. Photograph by Hélène Adant.
