Mi-le Buddha

China
Mi-le Buddha
Tang dynasty (A.D. 618–906), dated 705

Limestone
82.3 x 33.0 x 30.2 cm (32.4 x 12.9 x 11.9 in.)
Gift of Alice Getty, 1924.115

The image of the seated Buddha was adopted in China from India and may have originated in portraits of Indian royalty. In China, this posture was reserved for images of Mi-le, the Buddha of the Future, whose arrival was predicted in Buddhist scriptures. The cranial mound (ushnisha), covered here with tightly curled hair, symbolizes enlightenment and is specific to images of the Mi-le Buddha. However, other features on this figure are common to several Buddhist deities. The concave forehead circle (urna) is an auspicious mark from which wisdom radiates. Elongated earlobes allude to the aristocratic Indian custom of wearing heavy earrings and thereby to the origin of the historical Buddha as an Indian prince. The symbolic hand gesture (mudra) of his right (now broken) hand, with palm facing outward, signifies “fear not.”

The deity’s feet rest on lotus flowers, and lotus petals encircle his inner halo. As the lotus’s pure white petals rise from muddy water, the flower evokes Buddhist beliefs in purity and the search for enlightenment. Flames, indicating Mi-le’s radiance, border the outer halo.

The inscription on the base states that in 705 this sculpture and two bodhisattvas, or saintly beings, were dedicated by a Buddhist disciple in honor his deceased parents, seven generations of his ancestors, and his living relatives. The bodhisattvas most likely were created as separate sculptures, whose present whereabouts are unknown.

China
Bodhisattvas
Tang dynasty (A.D. 618–907), c. 725/50

Limestone with traces of polychromy
157.5 cm (62 in.)
Lucy Maud Buckingham Collection, 1930.84a, 1930.85

In the Buddhist faith, bodhisattvas are divine and compassionate beings who are destined for future enlightenment but have postponed this goal so as to guide others along the same spiritual path. Whereas the Mi-le and other images of the Buddha are portrayed as austere and sternly formal, bodhisattvas often appear as living beings with idealized features. These two bodhisattvas share graceful proportions, hair elegantly upswept and crimped into a high topknot, flowing drapery and scarves, and elaborate jewelry. Both assume casual poses: one is seated cross-legged, and the other is poised to step down from the pedestal. Although originally brightly painted, only traces of pigment are preserved on their surfaces. The large scale of these figures suggests that they probably furnished a cave temple that was carved into the face of a mountain or cliff.
