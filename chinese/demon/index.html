Armored Guardian King (Tianwang) Trampling Demon

China
Armored Guardian King (Tianwang) Trampling Demon
Tang dynasty (618–907 A.D.), first half of 8th century

Earthenware with three color (sancai) lead glazes and traces of pigments
Height: 45 in.
Gift of Mrs. Bertha Palmer Thorne, 1970.1069

His mouth fiercely open, eyes bulging, and stance powerfully tense, this guardian vividly conveys his mighty victory over the small demon who struggles beneath his feet. The guardian’s strong, muscular arms and clenched fist once raised a weapon that has been lost. Armor embellished with stylized dragonheads protects his shoulders, and a highly detailed headdress of a phoenix with outstretched wings crowns his ornately coiffed hair. Such elaborate features highlight his supernatural power.

The source of this evil-conquering guardian is rooted in and beyond China. Guardians described in early Indian mythology protected the four directional quarters of the universe, and Buddhist devotees helped popularize this image throughout Central and East Asia. In China, guardian figures made of clay and stone protected secular and religious sites. In tombs of the wealthy and powerful, clay figures such as this one were often stationed near the burial chamber to protect the deceased from evil.

This tomb figure was created by assembling several hollow clay sections that were molded and hand-modeled. A white slip was then applied before coating the surface with fluid, lead-based glazes colored with metallic oxides. Vivid caramel, olive-green, and white or transparent glazes were applied to the sleeves, poured and allowed to flow freely over the armor and patterned by a base of wax or some type of paste to resist the caramel and green glazes on the base. Such polychrome glazes are generally known as “three color” or sancai. The face and hands were left unglazed and painted with pigments after firing, but those unfired pigments have flaked off over time and only small traces survive.

Seated Woman Holding Mirror

Earthenware with three-color (sancai) lead glazes and traces of polychrome pigments
31.2 x 13.8 x 15.5 cm (12.3 x 5.4 x 6.1 in.)
Gift of Pauline Palmer Wood, 1970.1075

China
Seated Women Holding Mirror
Tang dynasty (A.D. 618–907), first half of 8th century

Earthenware with three-color (sancai) lead glazes and traces of polychrome pigments
28.5 x 11.0 cm (11.2 x 4.3 in.)
Gift of Mrs. Janis H. Palmer, 1971.871

Upper-class members of Tang dynasty society were buried with clay figures that closely reflect their sophisticated and fashion-conscious lives. Gazing into small hand mirrors (one square, the other round and lobed), these women wear close-fitting, layered garments that were inspired by Persian and Central Asian styles. Whereas the young girl on the left has pigtails and wears a garment with a low-cut bodice, the other woman has styled her hair into an upswept, curled topknot and is dressed in a cap-sleeved jacket and long scarf over her softly draped skirt. Both appear to be absorbed in applying cosmetics, which during the 7th and 8th centuries included mineral pigments that were imported from Persia.

Before firing in the kiln, the garments of both figures were coated with polychrome glazes known in Chinese as sancai, literally meaning “three color.” Their faces, by contrast, were left unglazed and painted after firing. Those pigments are vulnerable to flaking and have disintegrated over many centuries. One may only imagine that their delicate facial features originally enhanced an air of feminine elegance for these aristocratic ladies.
