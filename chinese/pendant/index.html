Dragon Pendant

China
Dragon Pendant
Zhou period, 4th–3rd century B.C.

Jade
9.2 x 16.8 x 0.7 cm (3.6 x 6.6 x 0.3 in.)
Edward and Louise B. Sonnenschein Collection, 1950.640

There are a variety of stones that are collectively known as “jade” in the West. Nephrite, the primary jade stone seen in ancient Chinese objects, is found most commonly in boulders or small pebbles that weather in such a way as to camouflage their internal beauty. Hard, finely grained, and resistant to carving, jade must be worn away slowly and laboriously by an abrasive paste applied to its surface with drills, grinders, and rotary saws.

This jade pendant, crafted during the Warring States period of the Eastern Zhou dynasty, reflects the atmosphere of economic development, social mobility, political upheaval, and artistic brilliance of the time. Throughout this period, ornamental objects of unprecedented technical refinement and visual splendor overshadowed the ritual art that flourished during the Shang dynasty.

This image of a dragon, one of a pair, is pierced through its arched hindquarters to be suspended as a pendant. It was probably part of an elaborate set worn as a necklace by its owner. The creature’s undulating trunk, as well as its nose, horn, and lower jaw, are all textured with rounded, c-shaped curls. The development and mastery of progressively sturdier rotary tools enabled jade carvers to achieve intricate surface designs that rival those found on contemporary bronze objects. When buried with the deceased, jade pendants such as these may have reflected widely held beliefs in the dragon as a vehicle for the human soul on its journey to the netherworld.

China
Wine Vessel (Hu)
Western Han dynasty, 2nd/1st century B.C.

Bronze with incised decoration and gold and silver amalgams
Height: 46.0 cm (18.1 in.); Diameter: 36.0 cm (14.2 in.)
Lucy Maud Buckingham Collection, 1927.315

The supernatural dragon became prominent in the Han dynasty as a creature associated with both imperial authority and the ever-changing nature of the universe. The dragon was thought to be endowed with the same dynamic energy that animates clouds and vapors. This vessel depicts intertwined dragons, whose trunks flow into scrolling, cloudlike patterns to create continuous bands of ornament.

These fluid designs were first incised into the bronze after casting. Bits of gold and silver were dissolved in separate solutions of hot mercury and were then painted onto the bronze. Finally, the vessel was reheated to set the designs. The mercury evaporated during firing, creating a two-toned surface that could be burnished to a high sheen.
