Man’s Jifu (Semiformal Court Robe)

China
Man’s Jifu (Semiformal Court Robe)
Qing dynasty (1644–1911), 1750/75

Silk and gold-leaf-over-lacquered-paper-strip-wrapped silk
149 x 211 cm (58 5/8 x 83 in.)
Gift of the Antiquarian Society, 1912.1805

A Chinese tale relates that the wife of the legendary Yellow Emperor discovered silk around 3000 B.C. when a cocoon fell from a mulberry tree into her hot tea and unraveled into silk fibers. By the Han dynasty (206 B.C.–A.D. 220), silk was China’s most luxurious, practical, and marketable commodity—a material used for painting as well as clothing that was both warm and beautiful. Silk had also become a major form of currency. The material was used to pay taxes, wages of government officials, and compensation to foreign tribes for their purebred horses.

In imperial China, the design of a silk robe often displayed the status and virtues of the wearer. Along the hem of this robe, mountain peaks rise above turbulent waves, a motif that carried many allusions, including the ability of the ruler to tower over his subjects. Dragons symbolize the dynamic energy of the universe. (Although five-clawed dragons, seen on this jifu, were often associated with the emperor, they were not exclusively imperial motifs.)

Delicately drawn floral motifs set against the gold background in the upper portion of the robe and in the hem represent the seasons. Some of these flowers evoke ideals of longevity and immortality that are associated with Taoism. Set among the flowers are white shapes that represent bats, a symbol of blessings or riches. This association can be traced to the fact that when spoken, the Chinese word for "bat" sounds the same as the word for "blessings" or "riches." (See the diagram to locate the various symbols.)

China
Brush Holder Depicting a Taoist Paradise
Qing dynasty, Qianlong period (1736–95), dated 1795

Jade
Height: 17.8 cm (45.2 in.); Diameter: 16.5 cm (41.9 in.)
Bequest of Grace Brown Palmer, 1967.88

This luxurious vessel was made to hold brushes for painting and calligraphy. It is crafted from a rich green stone known as “spinach jade,” which is typically light to medium green in color with small black flecks of graphite. Encircling the vessel is a continuous scene of figures that dwell in a mountainous, tree-laden landscape. They represent legendary beings that are distinctive to ancient Chinese beliefs in immortality. Many such “immortals” were later integrated into the popular folklore of the Taoism. Fundamental to Taoism is the idea of sacred peaks. Mountains are understood as points that connect heaven and earth and are important places of meditation. Taoist immortals began their lives as humans and through self-discipline and learning transform spiritually and physically into gods.

Inscribed around the upper rim of this vessel is a poem attributed to the Qianlong Emperor (reigned 1736–1795):

Originating with a single vein from [Mount] Kunlun, ringed with wutong trees
It flowed into Khotan and gave birth to the essence of jade.
Looking at this, can one determine the maker?
From the northern valleys of Western Liang, the immortal’s spirit is transmitted.

Although “Western Liang” was the name of a historical dynasty, its reference here is purely imaginary. Similarly, the wutong, or phoenix tree, is a natural species of tree with mythical associations: the phoenix bird was believed to perch on this tree and symbolize the peaceful rule of a virtuous emperor. “Kunlun” refers to a mountain range in far western China, which in Taoist belief is regarded as sacred. Nearby lay the Central Asian oasis kingdom of Khotan, which was a major source of raw jade for centuries in China. Jade production reached its pinnacle during the Qing dynasty (1644–1911). New mines and better quarrying made large quantities available in a wide variety of colors.
