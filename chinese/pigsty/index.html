Pigsty and Latrines

China; probably from Henan province
Pigsty and Latrines
Han dynasty (206 B.C.–A.D. 220), 1st century B.C./A.D.

Gray earthenware with stamped decoration
8 1/2 x 11 x 16 in.
Gift of Mrs. Bertha Palmer Thorne, 1971.867

This earthenware model of a pigsty presents an image of everyday life during the Han dynasty (206 B.C.–A.D. 220), when burial customs spread beyond the wealthy and powerful to middle-class government officials, landowners, and farmers. They commissioned modest objects exclusively for burial. Such objects reflected contemporary beliefs that the human soul was dual in nature and that its parts separated upon death. Whereas the ethereal soul (hun) ascended to the realm of the ancestors, the physical soul (po) remained close to the body and sought comfort in familiar surroundings.

This realistic model of a pigsty and latrines clearly served the needs of the physical soul. Here, young piglets suckle on a mother pig that rests near a rectangular trough. Above this trough, a semicircular latrine projects out of the back wall. This construction facilitated recycling: as pigs consumed human waste, their own waste was used to fertilize crops that fed a growing population.

Parallel lines incised in the walls of the pigsty depict layers of stamped earth, which would have been shoveled between boards and beaten in successive layers to create cement-like hardness. Until recently, this was a primary means of construction in China.

Winged Beast (Tomb Figure)
Western Han dynasty (206 B.C.–9 A.D.)

Gray earthenware, slip-coated with polychrome pigments
22.2 x 43.8 cm (14 7/8 x 27 1/2 in.)
Gift of Stanley Herzman in memory of Gladys Wolfson Herzman; Avery Brundage, Louise Lutz and Russell Tyson Endowments, 1997.337

Images made to furnish underground tombs were imaginative as well as realistic. This winged beast combines a wolf-like head, tautly arched body with long, ribbonlike wings, and short, powerfully modeled legs. The hollow figure was shaped in clay molds and then refined by hard tooling. Once fired, the surface was painted with scales, muscles, and tufts of fur.

Fantastic, protective beasts such as this clay sculpture derived from earlier forms in bronze. Similar creatures grew to monumental proportions in stone figures that were lined the ceremonial paths approaching grave mounds.

China
Funerary Urn (Hunping)
Western Jin dynasty (A.D. 265–316), late 3rd century

Stoneware with olive-green glaze and molded and applied decoration
Height: 48.7 cm (19.2 in.); Diameter: 27.5 cm (10.8 in.)
Through prior bequests of Mary Hooker Dole and Grace Brown Palmer; through prior gifts of Josephine P. Albright in memory of Alice Higinbotham Patterson, and Mrs. Kent S. Clow; Russell Tyson, Robert C. Ross endowments, 1987.242

This complex vessel is known as an “urn of the soul” (hunping) because it is believed to have served as a symbolic dwelling for the spiritual soul of the deceased. Its gateways and towers, guards with spears, monster mask ring-handles, and a combination of real and mystical creatures all derive from the funerary art of the previous Han dynasty. In the lowest rim a tortoise supports a tall funerary tablet or memorial stone. Nearby is a small image of the Buddha, who sits cross-legged with his hands folded in meditation. His hair is coiffed into a topknot, or ushnisha, which signifies his enlightenment. The minor role of such Buddhist imagery in the art of this period reflects an early stage of the integration of the Indian religion into Chinese culture. During the late third century, the Chinese may have understood this religion only in the context of their own funerary traditions and thus interpreted Buddhist images as auspicious charms that might serve to protect the deceased.

The opaque, olive glaze that covers this vessel would subsequently become more uniform in color and texture. In the West, such glazes are known as celadon.
