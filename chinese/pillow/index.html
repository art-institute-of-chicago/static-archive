Cloud-Shaped Pillow

China
Cloud-Shaped Pillow
Jin dynasty (1115–1234), 12th/13th century

Cizhou ware; stoneware, slip-coated with underglaze painting in iron brown slip
Gift of Russell Tyson, 1946.936

Since the Tang dynasty (618–907), the Chinese have valued ceramic pillows for daily use as well as for burial. These headrests are cool in the summer and comfortable throughout the year if covered with a thin mat.

Characteristically hollow, such pillows are made with small holes in the back to allow gases to vent during firing (and prevent the clay from exploding in the kiln). Chinese potters designed these objects in many shapes and embellished them with both pictorial and decorative designs. This cloud-shaped pillow with its foliate edge is skillfully painted with a wide-eyed carp that swims through strands of seaweed. The carpâ€™s long upper fin overlaps in front of a streamer of seaweed, while the tail fin is behind it. Traditional Chinese legends imbue this fish with many auspicious qualities. By swimming upstream against the strong currents of the Yellow River, carp came to symbolize steadfastness and perseverance. They also symbolized academic achievement—most notably success in the imperial civil service examinations.

This pillow represents a type of ceramic ware known as Cizhou, named after one of many centers of production in north China. Cizhou wares are distinguished by a creamy white slip (liquid clay) topped with a clear glaze but otherwise vary widely in decorative technique and style. Masterfully crafted pieces such as this pillow undoubtedly served a well-to-do clientele. When this object was made, north China had been conquered by non-Chinese tribesmen known as Jurchen, who founded the Jin dynasty. Jin aristocrats admired and actively supported Chinese craftsmanship, and the Cizhou kilns thrived under their patronage.
