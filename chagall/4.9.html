CHAGALL AND MARQ Making America Windows

Finally, Chagall and Marq reviewed the panels and adjusted and substituted different colors of glass as needed. Most importantly, Chagall painted details of his design in grisaille, a vitreous black paint made of glass flux and metallic oxides, which becomes permanent when fired. Traditionally, grisaille is applied in layers as matting (shading) or tracing (outlines) and fired between applications; Chagall, however, did all of his painting at once, adding heavier trace lines, stipples, and washes. Using different tools, and even his fingers, he worked the medium by scratching away the paint in some places down to the bare glass itself. Upon completion, each panel was taken out of its caming, the glass fired to fuse the grisaille, and then re-camed and puttied.
